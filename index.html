Brain Conditions Visualization Project

On this page we are going to explore several common brain diseases. Brain diseases are conditions that affect the brain, spinal cord, or nerves and disrupt how we think, move, feel, and function. They range from sudden events like stroke to chronic, progressive illnesses such as Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, multiple sclerosis, and epilepsy, as well as infections, tumors, traumatic brain injury, and headache disorders. Together, these conditions push a major global health burden across all ages, contributing to disability, mortality, and long-term caregiving needs. There are several factors that contribute to these brain diseases, and we are going to explore them throughout this page.

Global Brain Condition Breakdown by Type

Global Brain Condition Breakdown Pie Chart

This chart illustrates the global distribution of major brain conditions by type. Migraines account for nearly half of all reported neurological conditions, emphasizing their widespread prevalence across populations. Alzheimer’s disease and dementia represent the second-largest share, reflecting the growing impact of aging populations worldwide. Stroke and epilepsy follow, contributing significantly to neurological disability and mortality. Less common disorders such as multiple sclerosis, Parkinson’s disease, and other neurological conditions together make up a small but important portion of the overall disease burden. We will explore some of these diseases more in this page and look at demographics associated with them.

Alzheimer's Distribution by Gender and Race

Alzheimer's Race and Gender Bar Graph

This chart illustrates the distribution of Alzheimer’s disease cases by gender and race. White females represent the largest proportion of reported cases, highlighting both the higher prevalence among women and the demographic concentration in White populations. Black or African-American individuals show a smaller but notable share, consistent with studies suggesting varying diagnosis rates and access to care across racial groups. Male cases are significantly lower across all racial categories, reflecting the generally higher Alzheimer’s incidence in females. The visualization underscores the need for equitable diagnostic practices and inclusive research to better understand how gender and race influence Alzheimer’s risk and outcomes.

This world map illustrates the global distribution of Multiple Sclerosis (MS), with color intensity representing varying prevalence rates across regions. Higher rates of MS are concentrated in North America, Northern Europe, and Australia, indicated by the darker shades of blue. Lower prevalence is seen in regions closer to the equator and in developing countries, such as parts of Africa, South America, and Southeast Asia. This geographical pattern suggests a potential link between latitude, genetics, and environmental factors in influencing MS risk. Overall, the map provides a clear visual comparison of how MS affects populations unevenly across different parts of the world.

What is PHQ-9?

The PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire 9) is a short, 9-question survey used to measure depression severity. Higher numbers mean worse mental health. Each question corresponds to one of the DSM criteria for major depressive disorder and asks how often a person has experienced certain symptoms over the past two weeks (like little interest or pleasure in doing things, trouble sleeping, or feeling tired). Each item is scored from 0 to 3 for “not at all,” “several days,” “more than half the days,” or “nearly every day”. Giving a total score from 0 to 27.

Preventing Brain Diseases

There are so many factors we talked about here that impact many different types of brain diseases and physcial health issues. The goal here is to see how many different aspects of the way we live our lives can improve our chances at staying healthy for longer. Even though some risks come from factors out of our control, we can still work on proper diet, exercise, and mental and physical healthcare to greatly decrease susceptibility to brain diseases. These issues are ones to take on at an individual and societal level too. We should consider how equitable access is to these preventative measures and what we can do as a society to help everyone.

Based on the charts in our project, it’s clear that demographic factors play a major role in shaping the prevalence of brain conditions. Regional and global patterns reveal that factors such as age distribution, lifestyle, genetics, and healthcare access significantly influence disease rates like Alzheimer’s and Multiple Sclerosis. For instance, older populations in the South and Northeast U.S. show higher Alzheimer’s rates, while MS prevalence is greater in higher-latitude countries with colder climates. These differences highlight how environmental exposure, diet, and socioeconomic conditions intersect with biological risk. Overall, understanding these demographic effects is essential for developing targeted prevention, awareness, and healthcare strategies that address the unique needs of each population.
